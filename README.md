# A Guide to Physical Science: Physics

## About This Guide

This guide to the Regents Examination in Physical Science: Physics is designed to be as brief as possible while still serving as a well-crafted outline for the Physical Science: Physics course.

This examination was administered for the first time on June 17, 2026 in many public high schools across New York State. However, due to the lack of adequate public resources available for practice, many students took the exam with only minimal knowledge of the format and relative difficulty of the questions. Furthermore, many educators reported that the course was difficult to adapt to, as they were also provided few details about which topics from the old Regents Examination in Physical Setting / Physics are excluded in the new course.

This guide hopes to bridge the gap between the old course and this new cluster-based examination. It is created by a former student who took this course in the 2025–2026 term and has firsthand experience and knowledge about the topics included in this course.

## The Cluster-Based Format

The new Regents Examination in Physical Science: Physics is organized in a cluster-based format, and the exam length may vary throughout the years. This new format tests students more on critical thinking and connection skills rather than straightforward concept recall abilites.

Each cluster is a set of around five questions, each of which are based on a central theme or real-world scenario. A large portion of the exam is still multiple-choice questions; however, short-answer and free-response questions are more complex and may require combining ideas from multiple topics or concepts.

The test is heavy in argument-based questions, which usually ask students to support or refute a claim and provide evidence and reasoning to justify their response. To justify their response, a student would usually be required to demonstrate their understanding by creating a mathematical model, a drawing, or explaining, in full sentences, evidence that clearly supports or refutes the given claim.

Additionally, each question may build upon previously mentioned ideas by incorporating new diagrams or texts. Oftentimes, not all of the information provided may be used.

## Guide Key

The following symbols may be used throughout this guide to indicate certain ideas:
- Asterisk (*): The idea or topic (and its subtopics) were included in the old Physical Setting / Physics course but not in the new Physical Science: Physics course.
- Double Asterisk (**): The idea or topic (and its subtopics) are a major aspect of the new Physical Science: Physics course.
